Portfolio Research

November 18th, 2020

What makes for a good design portfolio site?

For inspiration I looked at 3 Portfolio sites for inspiration: 1) The Sartorialist, 2) !!! (Chk Chk Chk) Band Website, and Blank Banshee’s band page. I felt that all three sites had their own unique design language that reflected the topics they covered. The general design aspects I found in each of these sites include having: 1) a navigation bar on the top portion of the window, 2) a unique typeface for titles and menus, 3) an icon or logo that is present in both the banner and tab bar, 4) consistent design queues between the pages, and external links to their social media sites and contact pages. HTML Deep Dive

November 15th, 2020

After taking a deep dive in HTML, I found three cool aspects of the language that I would like to share. I hope to include these elements in future updates of my webpages. The three cool tidbits of HTML I found that were cool are: 1) abbr, 2) small, and 3) sub.

With abbr you can denote an abbreviation or acronym on your web page. As an added bonus of functionality, you have the option to include a “title” attribute to provide an expansion or description for the abbreviation that will pop up when you place your cursor over the abbreviated word. For example, by stating the "title" for the abbreviated LOL as "laugh out loud", LOL would provide a pop up text “laugh out loud” when the user places a cursor over LOL. This element will be very helpful when I get around to creating a web page for my master’s research; I have a tendency to use a lot of abbreviations in my research writings. Read More

History of the Internet

October 5th, 2020

The decade I chose to research was the 1990's where I found two interesting pieces relating to the internet.

The first piece I found interesting was with a time period called "the Eternal Summer", which started in September 1993. This event occurred when AOL started offering Usenet, an early messaging board system originally limited to colleges and research institutions, access to its many non-academic users. This growing popularity in AOL's Usernet service led the company to further distributing "millions of floppy disks and CD-ROMs with free trials". This helped spearhead the popularity of the internet with the general public in the mid-to-late 90's.
