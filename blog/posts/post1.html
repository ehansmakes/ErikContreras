Blog Post 1 - History of the Internet

October 5th, 2020

The decade I chose to research was the 1990's where I found two interesting pieces relating to the internet.

The first piece I found interesting was with a time period called "the Eternal Summer", which started in September 1993. This event occurred when AOL started offering Usenet, an early messaging board system originally limited to colleges and research institutions, access to its many non-academic users. This growing popularity in AOL's Usernet service led the company to further distributing "millions of floppy disks and CD-ROMs with free trials"[1]. This helped spearhead the popularity of the internet with the general public in the mid-to-late 90's. This would explain why there are so many AOL disks floating around my parent's house and thrift stores.

The second piece I found interesting was the use of Bulletin Board Systems (or BBS) in the early-to-mid 90's. BBSes acted as a precursor to the modern form of the internet where users could talk to each other via chatrooms, and compete with each other in online games. In 1994 "there were 60,000 BBSes serving 17 million users in the United States alone" (according to InfoWorld)." [2] The introduction of inexpensive dial-up internet service and the Mosaic web browser offered ease of use and global access that BBS and online systems did not provide, and led to a rapid crash in the market starting in 1994." [3]

Fun fact, there are BBS sites that are still running to this day. The oldest operational BBS site is Starfleet HQ, which has been running its "latest" release since 1994. The aesthetic of BBS sites is something I've seen before, but I never realized it was from an early form of the internet until now. I hope I could emulate similar designs in my future webpages given it bold color schemes and minimalistic approach to graphics. It makes me wonder if a minimalist approach is best given the variety of devices our webpages will have to be displayed on.
